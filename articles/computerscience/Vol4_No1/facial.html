Facial Recognition

Eileen Xu

Introduction

Facial recognition technology (FRT) is a type of biometric technology, or technology which processes information on the human body. Biometric technology is used to discern a person according to their biological characteristics. Examples include voice, fingerprint, and iris recognition.

Advantages that biometric technology have over other ID systems such as passwords are their higher security and convenience. A person’s biological traits are unique to them and can not be replicated with ease. Moreover, it takes less time to scan your face or fingerprint than to input a long password.

Facial recognition is when technology analyzes a human’s face to authenticate their identity. It’s used in cybersecurity, for instance, unlocking iPhones through FaceID. FRT is implemented in law enforcement and airport security as well. An arrestee’s photo is added to local, state, and federal facial recognition databases. Later on, a Person of Interest’s (POI) photo can be compared to mugshots in those databases in attempts to apprehend the perpetrator. At airports, travelers with biometric passports and facial recognition devices are increasing. Undoubtedly, FRT is spreading to be incorporated into our lives more and more.

How does facial recognition work?

FRT systems store faces, or data, that can be accessed. These systems do not all work the same way but they usually execute four steps:

Face detection

The system’s camera detects a face’s image. The image can be extracted from a photo or video in many different contexts and angles. The person can be alone or in a crowd, and the angle their face is at can vary. They could be staring ahead or in profile.

Face analysis

The system examines and analyzes the face’s image. It reads the face’s proportions and takes into account its major features, whether it be the length between the eyes, cheekbone shape, or lip contour. The system’s objective is to detect facial landmarks needed to decipher the person’s identity. Most FRT are dependent on 2D images rather than 3D ones because the images can be matched with others in a database or on the Internet.

Face conversion

The analog or non-digital information is converted to digital information. In other words, the face is converted to data. The face analysis in Step 2 is transmuted into a numerical code. That numerical code is known as the faceprint, or a digitally recorded depiction of a person’s face. It can be likened to a fingerprint.

Face matching

The generated faceprint is compared to others in the facial recognition database. The faceprint will most likely match an image in it because the FBI has over 640 million images of people. There’s a high chance that yours will be amongst them. Private companies such as Apple do not have a facial recognition database and a person’s Face ID is stored only in your phone’s chip. The FRT can produce a similarity score, too, which is feedback on how similar an image may be to the database’s image. However, there can be errors in face matching: false negatives and false positives. A false negative is when there’s no match even though there should be one. A false positive is when there’s a match, but it’s an incorrect one.

It can be difficult to determine what level of scrutiny is best when designing a security system using FRT, as lower scrutiny will produce more false negatives and vice versa. For example, in phone facial recognition, it’s better to have a false negative when the phone does not succeed in recognizing you several times than a false positive when the phone recognizes other people as you. A high false positive rate in phone facial recognition would unlock your phone to others, leading to privacy invasion.

Facial Recognition Algorithms

One facial recognition algorithm uses gradients. The face’s image is replaced with another that only has the characteristics most necessary in facial recognition. The pixels in the new image are then replaced with relative lighting representation, which displays a pixel’s brightness in contrast to others. Gradients enable the FRT to recognize a face in many lighting types.

Another algorithm is projection; it depicts a 2D image on a 3D model by wrapping the image around the model. More characteristics appear or become more apparent in the 3D representation. Projection does not need an ideal image with good lighting and such in order to work.

Facial Recognition Technology History

Facial recognition started in 1964 when Woodrow Wilson Bledsoe, along with Charles Bisson and Helen Chan Wolf, developed a measurement system that could systematize faces. Their work was funded by an anonymous intelligence agency and thus, it wasn’t revealed to the public. From the 1970s to the 1990s, law enforcement agencies created their own facial recognition systems. In the 1970s, they added other methods to determine a person’s identity such as lip thickness and hair color. Later on, in the 1990s, linear algebra was integrated into FRT. Important characteristics on a face can be represented with eigenvectors. 10 years later, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) collaborated to create a facial recognition database. In 2006, the Face Recognition Grand Challenge was launched to master FRT use in the U.S. In the time period between the 2010s and the modern-day, computers have advanced to the extent that FRT is present in our everyday lives.

Conclusion

In summary, facial recognition evidently has many advantages, but there are disadvantages to it as well. Its advantages include increased security, reduced crime, and more convenience. On the other hand, its disadvantages are privacy invasion, data misuse, and the chance that errors may wrongly incriminate you. There are times when it’s inconvenient, such as during the COVID-19 pandemic when everyone is wearing masks. With half the face covered, FRT and FaceID are rendered useless. Overall, advances in FRT in the future will be revolutionary, but we need to know how to protect ourselves from it, too.
