Non-Fungible Tokens

David Chen

Introduction

Cryptocurrency tokens and art projects sold via “non-fungible" tokens have become hot topics due to the great prices and volumes at which these assets are sold for. This technology has attracted a diverse community of talented engineers, artists, and investors alike. With varying reactions from the public. NFTs and other tokens are linked to only a controlled amount of accounts at any time. This scarcity attracts artists, who can set rules for “ownership" of their art, and investors, who see a limited supply and a potential to drive up demand. Such a complex system was created with the labor of countless developers, all working to make a trustworthy system in a world where nobody, not even the central government, can be trusted, and openness is a virtue. From here, we begin our discussion of what makes up the potential technology of the future: the blockchain.

Decrypting the Blockchain

Various projects, such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, use a blockchain system. This means that there are individual “blocks" of transactions and data that is “chained" to previous blocks via embedded identifiers linking each block to its “parent", or the block it is derived from. These identifiers are typically SHA256 cryptographic hashes that follow an established constraint. Due to the random nature of cryptography, randomized blocks need to be generated and checked to find ones with valid hashes that fit these limits, but once a block is found it is easy to get its hash and verify its legitimacy. Since all this effort requires great hardware and energy investment, nodes that “mine" a valid block are rewarded with some of the cryptocurrency being transferred, with some of that reward coming from transaction fees and some of it being generated within that block. The newly minted cryptocurrency ensures that the network’s economy will grow at a constant and established rate.

This proof-of-work system of hashes allows for data to be stored across a worldwide network of “node" computers without fears of tampering or corruption. Each block can be easily validated, but it takes a substantial amount of computing power to generate a valid block at all. To prevent individual nodes from falling too far out of sync, nodes are programmed to prefer the longest valid blockchain they can find on the network. If an entity wished to abuse these mechanisms to publish blocks with falsified data, they would need an astronomical amount of processing power, at least half of the entire network, to generate enough valid blocks to outpace the chains containing legitimate transactions. This is known as a 51% attack, but they are extremely uncommon, as volunteer miners attempt to avoid these scenarios by splitting their efforts into separate pools.

The core point of a decentralized (distributed) system of blockchain nodes is that no one person should be able to get full control over the data and alter it, as everyone else is free to enter the system and contribute to the forming of consensuses using their own computational resources. All the rules are open and agreed upon by the community, rather than forced upon the people by some elite power. If people strongly disagree on a change, they can fork the blockchain, creating their own independent copy of the network that abides by that community’s rules.

Why Ethereum’s Blockchain?

Ethereum is somewhat of a successor to the first major decentralized blockchain, Bitcoin. Funnily enough, its creator, Vitalik Buterin, discovered Bitcoin and decentralized services after his favorite World of Warcraft character class was downgraded by the developers. This showed him the issue with centralized systems: the rules can change at anytime due to factors completely out of the average user’s control. From there, Buterin discovered Bitcoin and went on to contribute to the founding of Bitcoin Magazine.

One of the main things distinguishing Ethereum from Bitcoin is the concept of “smart contracts", Turing-complete programs living on the Ethereum network that can automatically perform any function desired when conditions are met. This can be the exchange of cryptocurrency tokens between accounts, the storing of any data on the network, or the performing of calculations across the entire network. These benefit from the decentralized nature of the network, as once the smart contract is set up, nobody can tamper with or otherwise alter the code or the data on the blockchain. Of course, maintaining all this infrastructure costs money, so smart contracts require gas fees of ether cryptocurrency (on the Ethereum network) to pay miner nodes for the NFTs that rely on these mechanisms for generation and distribution.

How do NFTs work?

NFTs make use of the Ethereum smart contract system to manage transactions and transferring of token data between wallet addresses. The widely accepted EIP-721 standard defines the data structure of the tokens and the application programming interface (API) for the smart contracts. Each individual token is set up with a unique ID code and metadata, which can only be assigned to a set amount of wallet accounts at a time. Each token’s metadata describes what the token is for, with the EIP-721 standard requiring that name, description, and image fields be filled out with appropriate strings. The image field link to a respresentation of the token, but in many cases, this image is the only thing the token represents. Smart contracts are established on the Ethereum network to generate the NFTs and distribute them, along with managing later transactions involving the tokens. This includes functions like transferFrom(), which brokers a transfer of the token, along with events, like Approval(), which keep track of and execute code when a transaction has been approved. This granular control over transactions allows NFT creators to define rules, like extra commission fees paid to the original artist after multiple transfers. From these core concepts, a vast community of developers, investors, and other users, and various projects to go with it.

NFT Projects

CryptoPunks

One of the first examples of an NFT project on the Ethereum network is Larva Labs’s CryptoPunks. They used a smart contract to distribute unique tokens corresponding to different punks of varying properties. Initially, the images of the punks were stored externally on Larva Labs’ servers, with only identifier hashes attached to the NFTs. Later on, the engineers invested the time and resources to create a new contract (as the old one cannot be edited) that stores and displays the punk images entirely on the Ethereum network, ensuring that they would be preserved around the world rather than stored on one source server.

CryptoKitties

In 2017, CryptoKitties, a smart contract game of "breeding" collectible NFT cats, got wildly popular on the internet. Each NFT had a 256-bit genome that would be decoded into attributes, and these could be passed down to child CryptoKitties via breeding. People were so excited to breed and trade these collectibles that the Ethereum network as a whole was bogged down with traffic related to the game. This can be considered the first instance of NFTs making it into the mainstream, so much so that it exposed the Ethereum network’s inability to truly handle great amounts of traffic.

Bored Ape Yacht Club

The Bored Ape Yacht Club, much like its predecessors, is based on a limited supply of NFTs on computer-generated images, and its exclusivity in both supply and entry fee give it some form of prestige. The Bored Ape community is offered exclusive services via ownership of a Bored Ape token, such as a private community art board. This exclusiveness encourages an elitist culture, which shines the community in a negative light. This snobby attitude is backed even further by ownership of the NFT representing full copyrights over the image, making the owners of the NFTs even more concerned about the protection of their investment.

What do people think about them?

Supporters

Being based on the Ethereum blockchain, CryptoPunks and other NFT series are mainly bought and exchanged using ether (ETH). The high (and volatile) price of the ETH cryptocurrency itself have made investors eager to try and make large sums of money from exchanging such tokens. Citi Bank has invested over $100,000 on a CryptoPunk NFT. On many other occassions, art pieces have been exchanged for millions of U.S. dollars in ETH, and many more go for thousands. A notable example of this would be the digital artist Beeple and his NFT collage of 5,000 pieces of his artwork, sold through Christie’s Auction House for over $69 million. These large valuations also make them attractive to those who wish to show off, as possessing one shows great financial status and makes the purchaser part of an exclusive and elite community of NFT owners.

Critiques

Due to the computationally expensive proof-of-work algorithms Ethereum uses to verify transactions, many node operators will consume great amounts of hardware and electricity to run their mining operations. Although some of the energy is obtained from renewable sources like hydroelectric dams, this use puts pressure on the power grids for communities that may end up paying more on their power bills because someone outside of the community wanted to make profit. Others use non-renewable fossil fuels, which environmentalists see as wasteful and harmful for the environment. The use of advanced mining hardware, either from chips with dedicated designs (ASICS), or from consumer-grade GPUs, puts a strain on the silicon supply, which is currently undergoing a crisis due to the supply chain blockage. Many in the personal computing and gaming industry have been affected by this overwhelming demand for hardware that will only serve to help miners profit. Nvidia, a popular gaming graphics card chip designer, has gone so far as to reduce their gaming hardware’s efficiency for Ethereum mining, and instead sell dedicated mining cards so as to keep the gaming card supply stable. Overall, many see cryptocurrency as a parasite on society, leeching off resources purely for profit.

These environmental, economical, and social costs all give cryptocurrency a bad reputation, and the behavior of many in the crypto community does not help. Various NFT owners are extremely possessive of their NFTs, going as far as to threaten those who screenshot, copy, or “Right Click > Save" their images with legal action. Those who bootleg NFTs do it because the idea of spending hundreds, if not thousands, of dollars on ownership of a digital image is ridiculous when images can be freely downloaded over the internet. Other "crypto-bros," as they are mockingly are seen as mere shills for crypto; they perform pump and dump schemes where they sell large volumes of NFTs by marketing them well, then disappear after making a profit. Even worse, some malicious actors have minted NFTs of artwork they do not own, making money off of stolen work. Many have made memes mocking the environmental impact of using cryptocurrency systems for such feeble and greedy matters, further reducing the reputation of crypto.

The future of crypto

Love them or hate them, blockchain technology and NFTs are here to stay. The concepts have been implemented in decentralized and open-source manners that allow anybody to start their own blockchain or help maintain an existing one. Ethereum has been working towards a “web3" concept, where websites are stored on peer-to-peer systems like the Ethereum network so no one can block, censor, or limit the freedoms and expressions of users. NFTs would be the main manner of keeping user assets.

Meta has plans to use NFTs on their own platforms to represent virtual objects and properties, and various other tech startups have already done so in a bid to help create the metaverse.

Conclusion

Overall, NFTs are a novel technology with the potential to represent major things. However, the current climate of the crypto community is leaning towards more trivial matters, like images and personal financial gain, tarnishing the reputation of a technology full of potential. The environmental costs are still quite heavy, and in order to be sustainable, blockchains must move away from proof-of-work methods, and instead to less energy-consuming methods like proof-of-stake, which Ethereum intends to replace PoW with in the 2.0 revision. As we head further into the future, who knows what new, meaningful innovations will be made off of today’s concepts?
