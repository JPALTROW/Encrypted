Sophie Germain’s Identity

Vera Maganov

Introduction

Imagine you’re stuck on this difficult math problem in which you need to prove whether the expression is composite. Instead of spending hours multiplying each exponent and then adding, you could apply and replace values in the Sophie Germain Identity.

Example: Prove that 532 + 441 is either composite or prime.

A Brief Biography

Sophie Germain is one of the first women to receive an award in mathematics for her contributions to number theory and Fermat’s last theorem. Born on April 1st, 1776, she had spent the majority of the Reign of Terror hiding in her father’s library, where she taught herself mathematics, Latin, and Greek. During that time, Germain had come across L’Histoire des Mathématiques, and the inclusion of Archimedes’ death awakened her interest in studying geometry. Despite her parents’ protests and the social stigma, Sophie continued her studies, and as an adult, used pseudonyms to borrow lecture notes from the École Polytechnique, a prestigious, male-only academy. She later used her alias to contact Adrien-Marie Legendre and Carl Friedrich Gauss, the authors of Essai sur la théorie des nombres and Disquisitones Arithmeticae, respectively. In 1815, the Paris Academy of Sciences offered a prize for proof of Fermat’s Last Theorem, which demonstrates that xn + yn = zn has no integer solutions for n > 2 and x, y, z does not equal to 0, rekindling Sophie Germain’s interest in number theory. She discovered her identity formula in her explorations related to the theorem. On June 27th, 1831, Germain, unfortunately, passed away at the age of 55 after a long battle with breast cancer. Her work is still regarded as one of the most significant contributions to mathematics in history.

Proof:

We use this theorem for finding factors of integers of a certain form and math competitions. To break down and then prove the concept, let’s write down the full formula. a4 + 4b4 = (a2+2b2+2ab)(a2+2b2−2ab) To prove that this is a true statement, we can fully distribute the right hand side and check if the results match. (a2+2b2+2ab)(a2+2b2−2ab) = (a4+2a2b2−2a3b) + (2a2b2+4b4−4ab3) + (2a3b+4ab3−4a2b2) = a4 + 4b4

The left-hand side is equal to the right-hand side, so the statement is true.

We can also prove Sophie Germain identity by deriving factors. First, we can complete the square. a4 + 4b4 = a4 + 4a2b2 + 4b4 − 4a2b2 We need to keep 4a2b2 and  − 4a2b2to keep expression equal to a4 + 4b4. Second, we can factor the polynomial using DOTS (difference of two squares). (a4+4a2b2+4b4) − 4a2b2 = (a2+2b2)2 − (2ab)2 = (a2+2b2+2ab)(a2+2b2−2ab)

Application

Using this information, we can prove that 532 + 441 is a composite expression. A composite is a positive integer that has at least one divisor other than itself and 1. We can replace a4 and 4b4 with 532 and 441, respectively. a4 + 4b4 = 532 + 441 = (58)4 + 4(410)4 In this case, a = 58 and b = 410. Then, find the factors by using Sophie Germain identity. 532 + 441 = (58)4 + 4(410)4 = [516+2(4)20+2(58)(410)][516+2(420)−2(58)(48)] The factors are greater than 1, so the expression 532 + 441 is composite. We can use this formula to solve more complex problems, but the method is still the same.

In addition to proving whether an expression is composite, we can find a factor pair or the largest prime divisor. A prime is any natural number that is divisible by only 1 and the number itself. For example, find the largest prime factor of 1212 + 502. We can replace a4 and 4b4 with 1212 and 502, respectively. a4 + 4b4 = 1212 + 502 = 114 + 4(5)4 In this case, a will equal to 11, and b will be equal to 5. Then, find the factors by using Sophie Germain identity. 114 + 4(54) = [112+2(5)2+2(11)(5)][112+2(5)2−2(11)(5)] Then, calculate inside the brackets to find 2 of the expression’s factors. 112 + 2(5)2 + 2(11)(5)][112+2(5)2−2(11)(5)] = (121+50+110)(121+50−110) = (281)(61) The divisors of the expression 1212 + 502 are 281 and 61. 281 is the largest prime factor.

A More Complicated Problem

Now that we know the basics, we can use what we’ve learned to solve more complicated problems. For example (1987 AIME): [(104+324)(224+324)(344+324)(464+324)(584+324)]/[(44+324)(164+324)(284+324)(404+324)(524+324)] Each of the the terms is in form y4 + 324. We can fully factor out the form through the Sophie Germain Identity. y4 + 324 = y4 + 4(3)4 = [y2+2(3)2+2(3y)][y2+2(3)2−2(3y)] = (y2+6y+18)(y2−6y+18) = [y(y+6)+18][y(y−6)+18] Then, we can substitute y with each changing value, e.g. 10 and 22. The original expression becomes: [10(10+6)+18)(10(10−6)+18)(22(22+6)+18)(22(22−6)+18)(34(34+6)+18)(34(34−6)+18)(46(46+6)+18)(46(46−6)+18)(58(58+6)+18)(58(58−6)+18)]/[(4(4+6)+18)(4(4−6)+18)(16(16+6)+18)(16(16−6)+18)(28(28+6)+18)(28(28−6)+18)(40(40+6)+18)(40(40−6)+18)(52(52+6)+18)(52(52−6)+18)] Afterwards, fully simplify the terms. [(10(16)+18)(10(4)+18)(22(28)+18)(22(16)+18)(34(40)+18)(34(28)+18)(46(52)+18)(46(40)−18)(58(64)+18)(58(52)+18)]/[(4(10)+18)(4(−2)+18)(16(22)+18)(16(10)+18)(28(34)+18)(28(22)+18)(40(46)+18)(40(34)+18)(52(58)+18)(52(46)+18)] Most of the terms cancel each other out. 58(64) + 18 and 4(−2) + 18 are left. (58(64)+18)/(4(−2)+18) = 3730/10 = 373 The solution is 373.

Conclusion

Ultimately, Sophie Germain, a brilliant French mathematician, discovered a formula that helps find divisors of certain integers. Her simple identity theorem can be used to solve more complicated problems and is a useful in math competitions.
